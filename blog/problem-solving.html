Here's a story of how I problem solve my way through this sprint.



Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you:

were blocked on a simple problem:

  • what was the problem?

    For loops have been confusing and scary for me ever since the javascript café challenge. Think of for loops like instructions for the computer to do a specific task multiple times. Imagine you have 100 boxes and you want to find the box which has your laptop. For loops are instructions for the computer to look at all of those boxes and check for the laptop. So since I was not comfortable with using them until now I have been avoiding to use them whenever I can.

  • what problem-solving techniques did you use?

    I researched the syntax and understood how it works. I ended up thinking lets break down what I’m asked to do. I wrote down the code to do the process for one single item. Using the above example I wrote down the code to check one box for the missing laptop. Then slowly introduced other steps like letting the computer know there are 100 boxes, and then letting it know to check box 1 then box 2 then box 3 so on and so forth until it reaches box 100.

  • how did you feel throughout the process?

    Felt frustrated initially knowing that for loops are essential building block for JavaScript and that I was not comfortable with them. Once I broke down the process I felt like I knew what was going on in the background for the for loops.

  • what did you learn?

    The learning process introduced back in the initial sprints was actually exactly what I need to be doing when stuck on an issue. Writing down comments has helped as well to break down complex issues. It creates a roadmap in simple words how to solve the issue.

  • what was the problem?

    I got to the gradebook challenge and having to learn minor details on how to add thing sto objects, removing them from the object. Pulling items from another array to bring them to the object. Seemed very complex at first sight.

  • what problem-solving techniques did you use?

    Knowing to solve a single part of the puzzle at a time I started hardcoding one piece of the puzzle then rewriting the next level of complexity. Doing this really helped me shorten the code which was maybe 10 lines or so down to just 2 lines. This new code was not only short but could handle future changes to the initial dataset.

  • how did you feel throughout the process?

    Felt very good and could instantly feel that I was getting better and more familiar to do things with different data types. Felt like I can now take on more complex issues and ready for the next level.

  • what did you learn?

    I learnt that its best to stick to the learning process mentioned in the first few sprints. To break down a problem into smaller manageable portions and then try to solve those first.

Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes:

  • Pseudocode – very confident, now that I have used these throughout the challenge. Writing comments, writing a hardcoded version of the smaller issue then using that as a starting point to make it more flexible.

  • Rubber ducky method – used this method in the for of comments to write down what the issue is and how I might be able to navigate. Get an overall map of the process was good too

  • Reading error messages – feeling like I know much more than before. In saying that I have come across situations when I wrote something that was just wrong and the error message went kdsvjbl aos;dpjv. In those situations I just went back to the previous step and tried again to get back on track and get a readable error message. The error messages did help with figuring out what is expected of the code and what my code was returning.

  • Console.logging – this one has been an absolute saviour in the later challenges. Used this after I wrote literally every single line.

  • Googling – helps to see different articles on the subject to find one that clicks. MDN documents are good but feel like they are slightly too in depth. So will read those but also supplement other materials for learning.

  • Asking your peers for help – helped a lot actually. Looked and asked for some people in my homegroup for help. Helped out a lot when I was stuck on something to get a very overall look at where I am stuck not just the written code solution.

  • Asking coaches for help – coaches are really good. I read the threads and from what I have seen they have managed to solve every single issue anyone has come up with.

  • Improving your process with reflection – reflection is a good reassurance method of what I know. Majority of times it makes me feel good about something and reassures me that I have learnt the stuff and am on the home-stretch.

Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help. Consider what made you reluctant to do so. What might you try differently next time?

In this sprint I have been stuck on multiple occasions when I didn’t really ask for help from peers. My main system is I try to google the issue. If no luck, I take a little break and come back to take another stab. If still no luck, I check if I am going over the timebox suggested time, if I am within the limit I try to take my time to understand the topics in further depth. If still no luck, I test out a bunch of different things, even if they seem silly and not correct I still try. After doing all these I’ve managed to solve my issues myself.

Mostly I do this because I want to understand the concepts and get familiar with the syntax and when to use them, I do want to do that trial and error myself rather than asking for help to point in the right direction.

Biggest factor is how much time I have spent being stuck and how much time I have left according to the timebox suggestion. If I feel like I’m falling behind – I’ll reach out for help.
